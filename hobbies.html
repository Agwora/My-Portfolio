The Human
Behind the Data

The best data scientists don't just write code. They cultivate curiosity everywhere — at the chessboard, in the charts, on the court, and between the pages of research papers. Here's how my hobbies shape how I think about data, models, and uncertainty.

🎧
Music Chart Analysis

When Burna Boy jumps 14 spots overnight, I don't just stream — I ask why. Playlist placement? TikTok trend? Radio add? A new music video? I've built small Python scripts to pull chart data from Spotify and Apple Music APIs, looking for statistical anomalies in streaming velocity.

🧠 Skill transfer → Hypothesis generation · Anomaly detection · API thinking
♟️
Chess & Strategy

Rated 1400 on Lichess. Every position is a probability tree — what's my best move given what my opponent might do? Chess taught me to calculate branching outcomes, sacrifice short-term advantage for structural superiority, and recognize when to simplify complexity.

🧠 Skill transfer → Decision trees · Trade-off analysis · Positional thinking · Minimax reasoning
📖
Behavioral Economics

Kahneman, Thaler, Ariely — I read them because data without human context is just decoration. Understanding cognitive biases (anchoring, availability heuristic, loss aversion) makes me a better analyst of human-generated data. People don't behave like rational agents, and good models account for that.

🧠 Skill transfer → Bias detection · Causal inference · User behavior modeling · Survey design
🏀
Basketball Analytics

Box scores lie. PER is incomplete without usage rate and opponent adjustment. The NBA analytics revolution taught me: always ask what a metric leaves out. I follow the work of Kirk Goldsberry, Darryl Morey, and the stats behind the modern game.

🧠 Skill transfer → Metric design · Context awareness · Skepticism of surface stats · Efficiency vs volume
📄
Reading Research Papers

Every week I read 1-2 papers from ArXiv, ACL, or ICML proceedings. Recent favorites: 'Attention is All You Need' (original Transformer paper), 'Dropout as a Bayesian Approximation', and 'RAG for Open-Domain Question Answering'. I implement small-scale versions of what I learn — it's how I stay current without waiting for courses.

🧠 Skill transfer → Continuous learning · Literature synthesis · Implementation from scratch · Staying current
